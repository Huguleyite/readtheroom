# Pundit — Game Spec
*Read the Room · Game 2*
*Spec completed: May 2026*

---

## Concept

**The Talking Head.** You're being profiled for a cable news gig. Answer 10 questions drawn randomly from a bank of 25 — each one presents a political situation and asks how you'd frame it on air. No labels. No axis visible. Pick what feels natural. Get assigned a pundit archetype.

---

## Structure

- **Question bank:** 25 questions total
- **Per session:** 10 drawn randomly at session start, locked for that play
- **Replay:** New random draw each time — meaningful variety across 3+ playthroughs
- **Answers per question:** 3 choices
- **Axes:** Two — measured invisibly across all answers

---

## The Two Axes

| Axis | Range | Zones |
|---|---|---|
| **Political Lean** (Left ↔ Right) | -10 to +10 | Left (≤ -3) · Center (-2 to +2) · Right (≥ +3) |
| **Delivery Style** (Calm ↔ Combative) | -10 to +10 | Calm (≤ -3) · Moderate (-2 to +2) · Combative (≥ +3) |

Each answer carries hidden values for both axes simultaneously. Players never see the scores.

---

## The 9 Archetypes

| | Left | Center | Right |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Calm** | The Wonk | The Explainer | The Traditionalist |
| **Moderate** | The Advocate | The Analyst | The Commentator |
| **Combative** | The Firebrand | The Referee | The Instigator |

---

## Archetype Hooks
*(Displayed on result screen — the shareability line)*

**The Wonk** — *"You have a 47-slide deck on this. Nobody asked for it. You're sharing it anyway."*

**The Advocate** — *"You believe in the cause. You also believe in a reasonable bedtime."*

**The Firebrand** — *"You came to talk. You also came to fight. It's fine — same thing."*

**The Explainer** — *"You have been asked to please take a side. You have politely declined."*

**The Analyst** — *"You see both sides. Your Thanksgiving table does not thank you."*

**The Referee** — *"You're not partisan. You're just loud about everyone being wrong."*

**The Traditionalist** — *"You prefer things the way they were. You're not sure exactly when that was, but it was better."*

**The Commentator** — *"You have opinions. They're measured. Your producer wishes they weren't."*

**The Instigator** — *"You are not here to make friends. You are here to make a point. And a scene. Mostly a scene."*

## The 25 Questions

---

**Q1 — Supreme Court Ruling**
The Court issues a 5-4 decision striking down a federal environmental regulation. You're live in 30 seconds. What's your lead?

- A. *"Today's ruling raises real questions about how far regulatory agencies can go without Congress. That's the conversation now."*
- B. *"The Court just handed polluters a blank check and called it constitutional law. This is what a captured court does."*
- C. *"Five to four. That margin should tell you everything about where we are right now."*

---

**Q2 — Tech CEO Testimony**
A major tech CEO spends six hours before Congress answering questions about data privacy. Your take?

- A. *"Six hours of grandstanding by legislators who can't explain what an algorithm does. Nobody won in that room."*
- B. *"Big Tech has been collecting your data, shaping your feed, and writing its own rules. Today was overdue."*
- C. *"This is what happens when Washington decides competition is a threat. More hearings, less innovation."*

---

**Q3 — Social Media Ban**
A major platform permanently bans a sitting senator for repeated policy violations. How do you frame it?

- A. *"A private company enforcing its own terms of service. This is not censorship — it's a contract."*
- B. *"Whether you agree with the senator or not, a corporation deciding who gets a political voice should make everyone pause."*
- C. *"Big Tech just decided which senator gets to speak. That's not a company decision — that's a power grab."*

---

**Q4 — Party Switch**
A three-term House member from a purple district switches parties eight weeks before an election. Your take?

- A. *"This is a political calculation dressed up as a conviction. The district will remember."*
- B. *"Parties change. People change. The voters in that district are the only opinion that matters here."*
- C. *"When your party stops serving your constituents, you have a choice to make. This lawmaker made one."*

---

**Q5 — Whistleblower Leak**
A federal employee leaks documents showing the government surveilled domestic protesters without warrants. What do you tell your audience?

- A. *"This is exactly why whistleblower protections exist. The public had a right to know."*
- B. *"Leaking classified documents is a crime, regardless of what's in them. You don't get to choose which laws apply to you."*
- C. *"The story isn't the leak. The story is what the government was doing while nobody was watching."*

---

**Q6 — Celebrity Endorsement**
An A-list actor endorses a presidential candidate at a sold-out arena event. Two days of wall-to-wall coverage follow. What do you say?

- A. *"Celebrities are citizens. They have every right to endorse candidates. The outrage is the real story."*
- B. *"When a campaign spends two days on a celebrity instead of policy, that tells you something about the campaign."*
- C. *"Hollywood telling working Americans who to vote for. This is exactly the elite disconnect that keeps losing elections."*

---

**Q7 — Debate Moderator Bias**
After a presidential debate, one campaign formally accuses the moderators of bias — citing 23 specific interruptions. Your read?

- A. *"Moderating a presidential debate is one of the hardest jobs in media. One campaign complaining after a loss isn't evidence."*
- B. *"Twenty-three is a specific number. Someone sat down and counted. That's not spin — that's documentation."*
- C. *"The format has been broken for decades. The networks profit. The voters don't. This is a symptom."*

---

**Q8 — Foreign Government Criticism**
A major allied country's prime minister publicly criticizes U.S. domestic policy at a press conference. How do you respond?

- A. *"When allies feel compelled to go public rather than private, it's worth asking why."*
- B. *"Foreign leaders don't get a vote in American elections. Their opinion on our domestic policy ends at their border."*
- C. *"There's always something worth hearing in an outside perspective, even when you disagree with the conclusion."*

---

**Q9 — Protest Standoff**
A protest outside a federal building becomes a 16-hour standoff. No injuries. Forty arrests. How do you lead?

- A. *"Forty arrests. That's the number. Everything else is context."*
- B. *"The right to protest does not include the right to occupy a federal building. There's a line, and it was crossed."*
- C. *"Sixteen hours of people demanding to be heard, and the headline is forty arrests. That framing is a choice."*

---

**Q10 — Mayor Defies State Law**
A major city's mayor announces the city will not enforce a new state law restricting public demonstrations. Your take?

- A. *"Sanctuary cities, sanctuary protests — at some point, the rule of law is either the rule or it isn't."*
- B. *"A mayor who believes a law is unconstitutional has a duty to say so. The courts will settle it."*
- C. *"This is what federalism looks like under pressure. It's messy. It's supposed to be."*

---

**Q11 — Presidential Pardon**
The president issues a full pardon to a former cabinet official convicted of obstruction. How do you frame it?

- A. *"The pardon power exists. This president used it. Whether it was wise is a question for the voters."*
- B. *"Pardoning someone convicted of obstructing your own administration. The optics alone should be disqualifying."*
- C. *"The case was politically motivated from the start. The president saw through it and acted. That's leadership."*

---

**Q12 — Senator Refuses to Testify**
A sitting senator invokes executive privilege and refuses to appear before a Senate investigative committee. Your take?

- A. *"Executive privilege has limits. A senator answering to a Senate committee shouldn't be one of them."*
- B. *"This is stonewalling dressed in legal language. The committee exists for a reason."*
- C. *"The precedent matters more than this particular hearing. Let the courts define the boundary."*

---

**Q13 — Government Shutdown**
The federal government shuts down at midnight after Congress fails to pass a funding bill. Day one. What's your lead?

- A. *"Workers aren't getting paid. Parks are closed. This is the cost of governing by crisis, and someone needs to say so."*
- B. *"A shutdown is a negotiation tool. Painful, yes. But sometimes the only leverage one side has left."*
- C. *"Both parties had months to avoid this. That's the story — not the shutdown, but the months before it."*

---

**Q14 — National Emergency Declaration**
The president declares a national emergency to redirect military funding toward border infrastructure. Your response?

- A. *"Declaring a national emergency to bypass Congress is the kind of precedent that doesn't go back in the bottle."*
- B. *"If Congress won't act, the executive will. The president made a call. That's the job."*
- C. *"Every president who expands executive power makes the next one more dangerous. That's the real conversation."*

---

**Q15 — Corruption Indictment**
A sitting congressman is indicted on federal bribery charges. He announces he will not resign. Your take?

- A. *"An indictment is not a conviction. He has the right to fight. The voters will ultimately decide."*
- B. *"Staying in office while under federal indictment is exactly why people hate politics."*
- C. *"Indicted and defiant. At least we're past the part where anyone pretends to be surprised."*

---

**Q16 — Ballot Measure**
A state ballot measure banning corporate political donations passes 51–49. How do you cover it?

- A. *"A slim majority is still a majority. The voters spoke. Now watch how fast the courts respond."*
- B. *"Fifty-one to forty-nine isn't a mandate. It's a coin flip with legal consequences."*
- C. *"Money in politics is the root of most of what's broken. If this holds up in court, it's a start."*

---

**Q17 — Tie-Breaking Vote**
The vice president casts a tie-breaking vote passing a major spending bill after three days of Senate deadlock. Your response?

- A. *"The system worked exactly as designed. A tie-breaking vote is a constitutional function, not a scandal."*
- B. *"One vote. One person. One agenda. This is why elections matter — and why this result is already being challenged."*
- C. *"Three days of deadlock settled by one unelected vote. Whatever side you're on, that's a fragile way to govern."*

---

**Q18 — Supreme Court Retirement**
A Supreme Court justice announces retirement, handing the current president a nomination. Your lead?

- A. *"The president will nominate. The Senate will advise and consent. That's how this works — and it's about to get loud."*
- B. *"The balance of the Court is shifting. Everything downstream — rights, regulations, elections — is on the table."*
- C. *"Another vacancy. Another confirmation fight. Another chance for Washington to remind the country why it's exhausted."*

---

**Q19 — Foreign Aid Package**
Congress passes a $14 billion foreign aid package over significant domestic infrastructure objections. How do you frame it?

- A. *"America's global commitments are investments in stability. The alternative is a more dangerous world that costs more."*
- B. *"Fourteen billion abroad while bridges are failing at home. Somebody explain that math."*
- C. *"Foreign policy has always been expensive. The question is whether we're getting what we paid for."*

---

**Q20 — Refusal to Concede**
A gubernatorial candidate loses by 1.8 points and refuses to concede, citing unspecified irregularities. Your take?

- A. *"One point eight is a loss. Calling it a conspiracy without evidence is how you poison an election."*
- B. *"Every candidate has the right to request a recount. The process exists. Let it run."*
- C. *"We've been here before. We know how it ends. The question is how much damage gets done on the way."*

---

**Q21 — Police Budget Reallocation**
A major city council votes to redirect 15 percent of its police budget toward mental health and housing programs. Your response?

- A. *"Fifteen percent is a policy choice, not abolition. The question is whether the alternatives are ready to deliver."*
- B. *"You don't solve a public safety crisis by defunding the people responsible for public safety."*
- C. *"The data on this is mixed. The data should lead — not the politics."*

---

**Q22 — Former President's Endorsement**
A former president endorses a primary challenger against a sitting member of his own party. Your take?

- A. *"Primaries are how parties shape themselves. A former president can weigh in. The voters decide."*
- B. *"Endorsing against your own party's incumbent is either a principled stand or a personal vendetta. The motive matters."*
- C. *"He just told you exactly what he thinks about the direction of the party. That's news."*

---

**Q23 — Independent Presidential Candidate**
A well-known independent enters the race polling at 12 percent nationally. How do you cover it?

- A. *"Twelve percent of voters saying neither party speaks to them. That's not a spoiler story — that's a crisis story."*
- B. *"Every four years the third-party moment arrives. Every four years the math doesn't change. Watch October."*
- C. *"A spoiler at 12 percent could hand this election to the wrong side. That's not a protest vote — that's a consequence."*

---

**Q24 — Last-Minute Vote Switch**
A senator switches their vote in the final hour, killing a major climate bill. No public explanation given. Your lead?

- A. *"No explanation. No statement. A bill dies and a senator walks. The voters in that state deserve an answer."*
- B. *"The bill couldn't hold its own coalition. If a senator couldn't support it at the end, maybe it wasn't ready."*
- C. *"Someone got to them in the last 24 hours. That's the story. Everything else is procedural."*

---

**Q25 — Rally Confrontation**
A political rally ends in a confrontation between attendees and counter-protesters. Minor injuries on both sides. How do you lead?

- A. *"Minor injuries, major symbolism. The temperature in this country doesn't go down on its own."*
- B. *"Both groups came looking for a fight and found one. That's not news — that's a pattern."*
- C. *"The right to rally and the right to counter-protest are both protected. What happened after that is the story."*
